Group Rings

and

The Unit Conjecture

  • Group rings are analogues of polynomials, or better still Laurent polynomials, i.e., finite sums $$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i x^{n_i}$$
  • We take $a_i$ to be elements of a field.
  • Observe that, by considering degrees, for two Laurent polynomials $p(x)$ and $q(x)$:
    • If $p(x)q(x)=0$, then $p(x)=0$ or $q(x)=0$.
    • If $p(x)q(x)=1$, then both $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are of the form $cx^d$, $c\neq 0$.

Group Rings

  • Fix a group $G$ and a ring $R$.
  • The group ring $R[G]$ is the set of formal sums $$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i g_i$$
  • We multiply bilinearly extending $G$: $$(\sum_{i=1}^k a_i g_i)\cdot (\sum_{j=1}^l b_j h_j) = \sum_{i=1}^k\sum_{j=1}^l (a_ib_j)(g_ih_j)$$

Why Group Rings

  • Any vector space $V$ over $k$ with a linear group of symmetries $G$ forms a $k[G]$-module.
  • Linear transformations respecting symmetries are given by matrices with entries in the group ring $k[G]$.
  • Many fundamental topological invariants, for example, are derived from these matrices.
  • We consider group rings over fields $k$.
  • If an element $g\in G$ is torsion, i.e., $g^n=e$ for some $n>1$, then we have $0$-divisors in $k[G]$ as $$(1-g)(1 + g + \dots + g^{n-1}) = 1 - g^n = 0.$$
  • Similarly, for many groups with torsion, $k[G]$ has non-trivial units, i.e., units not of the form $a\cdot g$.
  • Kaplansky conjectures: if $G$ is torsion-free, $k[G]$ has no zero divisors and no non-trivial units.
  • These are also related to fundamental questions in topology, K-theory etc.

Gardam's Theorem

  • Theorem (Gardam) Kaplansky's unit conjecture is false.
  • Thus,
    • For the field $k=\mathbb{F}_2$ with $2$ elements.
    • For a certain group $P$, called the Promislow or Hantzscheâ€“Wendt group.
    • We have elements $\alpha, \alpha'\in \mathbb{F}_2[P]$ with $\alpha\cdot\alpha'=1$.
    • $\alpha$ is not of the form $a\cdot g$.
    • $P$ is torsion free.
  • The group $P$ is a Metabelian Group, i.e., a group $G$ with an abelian normal subgroup $K$ so that the quotient $Q=G/K$ is also abelian.
  • As a set the Metabelian group $G$ is $G = K \times Q$.
  • As a group $G$ is determined by
    • The abelian groups $K$ and $Q$.
    • A group action of $Q$ on $K$, induced by conjugation in $G$.
    • A co-cycle $c: Q \times Q \to K$.
  • Torsion-freeness of $P$ follows from:
    • If $g\in P$ is a torsion element then so is $g^2$.
    • For all $g\in P$, $g^2=e$ implies $g=e$.
    • For all $g\in P$, $g^2 \in K$.
    • $K$ is torsion free.
  • As $\alpha$ and $\alpha'$ were given explicitly by Gardam, $\alpha\cdot\alpha'=1$ can be checked by a computation; provided the group ring is constructed in a manner that equality is Decidable.

Decidability:

Free modules, Enumeration, Bases

  • We construct free modules $R[X]$ so that
    • If equality is decidable for $R$ and $X$, it is for $R[X]$ – even when $X$ is infinite.
    • $R$-module homomorphisms on $R[X]$ are induced by functions on $X$.
  • We also use type-classes to automatically create decision procedures:
    • for properties on finite sets;
    • for equality of homomorphisms on finitely generated free abelian groups.
