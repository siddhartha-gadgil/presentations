Group Rings

and

The Unit Conjecture

  • Group rings are analogues of polynomials, or better still Laurent polynomials, i.e., finite sums $$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i x^{n_i}$$
  • We take $a_i$ to be elements of a field.
  • Observe that, by considering degrees, for two Laurent polynomials $p(x)$ and $q(x)$:
    • If $p(x)q(x)=0$, then $p(x)=0$ or $q(x)=0$.
    • If $p(x)q(x)=1$, then both $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are of the form $cx^d$, $c\neq 0$.

Group Rings

  • Fix a group $G$ and a ring $R$.
  • The group ring $R[G]$ is the set of formal sums $$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i g_i$$
  • We multiply bilinearly extending $G$: $$(\sum_{i=1}^k a_i g_i)\cdot (\sum_{j=1}^l b_j h_j) = \sum_{i=1}^k\sum_{j=1}^l (a_ib_j)(g_ih_j)$$

Why Group Rings

  • Any vector space $V$ over $k$ with a linear group of symmetries $G$ forms a $k[G]$-module.
  • Linear transformations respecting symmetries are given by matrices with entries in $k[G]$.
  • For example, chain groups of Galois covers of topological spaces give $k[G]$-modules.
  • Many fundamental topological invariants, for example, are derived from these matrices.
  • We consider group rings over fields $k$.
  • If an element $g\in G$ is torsion, i.e., $g^n=e$ for some $n>1$, then we have $0$-divisors in $k[G]$ as $$(1-g)(1 + g + \dots + g^{n-1}) = 1 - g^n = 0.$$
  • Similarly, for many groups with torsion, $k[G]$ has non-trivial units, i.e., units not of the form $a\cdot g$.
  • Kaplansky conjectures: if $G$ is torsion-free, $k[G]$ has no zero divisors and no non-trivial units.
  • These are also related to fundamental questions in topology, K-theory etc.

Gardam's Theorem

  • Theorem (Gardam) Kaplansky's unit conjecture is false.
  • More concretely:
    • For the field $k=\mathbb{F}_2$ with $2$ elements.
    • For a certain group $P$, called the Promislow or Hantzscheâ€“Wendt group.
    • We have elements $\alpha, \alpha'\in \mathbb{F}_2[P]$ with $\alpha\cdot\alpha'=1$.
    • $\alpha$ is not of the form $a\cdot g$.
    • $P$ is torsion free.
  • There are many descriptions of the group $P$ — indeed Gardam gave several.
  • One description is as the fundamental group of a $3$-manifold obtained by gluing two copies of the orientable twisted $I$-bundle over the Klein bottle.
  • The above gives a description as a Cystallographic group.
  • For our formalization, the most convenient is an explicit description as a Metabelian group.

Metabelian Groups

  • A Metabelian group is a group $G$ with
    • An abelian normal subgroup $K$,
    • so that the quotient $Q$ is also abelian.
  • The simplest examples of such groups are the products $K \times Q$.
  • The permutation group $S_3$ is metabelian with $K\cong\mathbb{Z}/3$ and $Q \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$.
  • The group $\mathbb{Z}$ has such a description with $K=2\mathbb{Z}$ and $Q\cong \mathbb{Z}/2$.
  • Let $G$ be a metabelian group with abelian normal subgroup $K$ and quotient $\varphi: G \to Q$.
  • By choosing coset representatives, we have a function $\sigma: Q \to G$ (called a section) so that $\varphi\circ\sigma$ is the identity on $Q$.
  • Each element in $G$ is uniquely of the form $k\cdot\sigma(q)$, giving an identification $G = K \times Q$ with $(k, q)\leftrightarrow k\cdot \sigma(q)$.
  • Choosing the section with $\sigma(0)$ the identity, we have $(k, 0)\cdot (k', 0) = (k + k', 0)$.
  • In cases like $S_3$, the section $\sigma$ can be chosen to be a homomorphism(split extensions).
  • In this case, we also have (identifying $\sigma(q)\equiv q$) $(0, q)\cdot (0, q') = (0, q + q')$.
  • By our identification $(k, 0)\cdot (0, q) = (k, q)$; however, we do not have $(0, q)\cdot (k, 0) = (k , q)$.
  • Instead we get a group action of $Q$ on $K$ given by $q\cdot k = (0, q)\cdot (k, 0) \cdot (0, q)^{-1}$.
  • Conversely, given a group action we get a group $G = K \times Q$ with product $(k, q)\cdot (k', q') = (k + q\cdot k', q + q')$.
  • As $K$ is abelian, we always get a group action $q\cdot k = \sigma(q)\cdot k\cdot\sigma(q)^{-1}$ independent of $\sigma$.
  • In general $\sigma$ is not a homomorphism.
  • We can measure the failure of $\sigma$ to be a homomorphism by considering $c(q, q') := \sigma(q + q')\cdot(\sigma(q)\sigma(q'))^{-1}$.
  • As $\sigma$ is a section, we deduce $c(q, q')\in K\subset G$, so $c: Q \times Q \to K$.
  • Associativity implies that $c$ is a cocyle.
  • Given a group action and a cocyle, we get a group structure on $K\times Q$ with product $(k, q)\cdot (k', q') = (k + q\cdot k' + c(q, q'), q+ q').$

The group $P$

  • The group $P$ is metabelian with $K\cong \mathbb{Z}^3$ and $Q\cong \mathbb{Z}/2\oplus\mathbb{Z}/2$.
  • Gardam gives an explicit description of the group action and the cocyle.
  • Thus, we need to formalize:
    • A general result that given $K$, $Q$, a group action and a cocycle we get a group.
    • In the case of $P$, the result that the given formulas give a group action and a cocycle.
  • Concrete descriptions are also used to prove torsion-freeness.
  • Torsion-freeness of $P$ follows from:
    • If $g\in P$ is a torsion element then so is $g^2$.
    • For all $g\in P$, $g^2 \in K \cong \mathbb{Z}^3$; which is torsion free.
    • For all $g\in P$, $g^2=e$ implies $g=e$; using explicit formulas for $g^2=(k, q)^2$ for the four values of $q\in Q\cong \mathbb{Z}/2\oplus\mathbb{Z}/2$.
  • As $\alpha$ and $\alpha'$ were given explicitly by Gardam, $\alpha\cdot\alpha'=1$ can be checked by a computation; provided the group ring is constructed in a manner that equality is Decidable.

Decidability:

Free modules, Enumeration, Bases

  • We construct free modules $R[X]$ so that
    • If equality is decidable for $R$ and $X$, it is for $R[X]$ – even when $X$ is infinite.
    • $R$-module homomorphisms on $R[X]$ are induced by functions on $X$.
  • We also use type-classes to automatically create decision procedures:
    • for properties on finite sets;
    • for equality of homomorphisms on finitely generated free abelian groups.
